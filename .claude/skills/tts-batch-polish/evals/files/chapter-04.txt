Chapter 4: Modern Consensus — Raft and Beyond

While Paxos solved the theoretical problem of consensus, its complexity
made it notoriously difficult to implement correctly. In 2014, Diego
Ongaro and John Ousterhout published the Raft protocol specifically
designed for understandability.

Raft decomposes consensus into three sub-problems:

1. Leader election: choosing a single node to coordinate
2. Log replication: the leader accepts commands and replicates them
3. Safety: ensuring that all nodes agree on the same log entries

The protocol uses a strong leader model. All client requests go through
the leader, which appends them to its log and replicates the entries
to followers. Once a majority of nodes have acknowledged an entry,
it is considered committed.

This approach trades some theoretical elegance for practical clarity.
Engineers can reason about Raft's behavior in terms of concrete state
machines rather than abstract mathematical properties.

Since Raft's publication, it has been adopted by numerous production
systems including etcd (used by Kubernetes), CockroachDB, and TiKV.
Its influence on the field has been substantial, demonstrating that
accessibility of algorithm design matters as much as correctness.
