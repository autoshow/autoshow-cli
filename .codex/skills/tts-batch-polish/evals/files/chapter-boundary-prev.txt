Chapter 5: Eventual Consistency

The CAP theorem, formulated by Eric Brewer in 2000 and proved by
Gilbert and Lynch in 2002, states that a distributed system can
provide at most two of the following three guarantees: consistency,
availability, and partition tolerance.

Since network partitions are inevitable in any real distributed system,
the practical choice is between consistency and availability. Systems
that choose availability over consistency are said to provide "eventual
consistency" — a guarantee that, given enough time without new updates,
all replicas will converge to the same value.

This model has proven remarkably useful in practice. Amazon's Dynamo
paper, published in 2007, demonstrated that eventual consistency could
support one of the world's largest e-commerce platforms. The key insight
was that many operations do not require strong consistency — a shopping
cart, for example, can tolerate briefly showing stale data as long as
no items are permanently lost.

sloppy quorums, and anti-entropy protocols — became the foundation for
an entire generation of NoSQL databases, including Cassandra, Riak, and
Voldemort. These systems prioritize availability and partition tolerance,
accepting that clients may occasionally read stale data in exchange for
