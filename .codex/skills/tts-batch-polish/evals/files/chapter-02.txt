Chapter 2: The Architecture of Distributed Systems

In 1978, Leslie Lamport published a paper that would fundamentally change
how we think about distributed computing.1 The paper, titled "Time, Clocks,
and the Ordering of Events in a Distributed System," introduced the concept
of logical clocks and the happens-before relation.

Lamport's key insight was that in a distributed system, the notion of
"simultaneous" events is meaningless unless the events are causally related.2
Two events at different nodes can only be ordered if one could have influenced
the other through message passing.

This was a radical departure from the centralized computing model that
dominated the field at the time. In a centralized system, a single global
clock provides a total ordering of all events. But in a distributed system,
no such global clock exists.

The implications were profound. If you cannot establish a total ordering
of events, then you cannot guarantee that all nodes in the system see the
same sequence of operations. This leads directly to the consistency
problems that plague distributed databases to this day.

1 Lamport, L. "Time, Clocks, and the Ordering of Events in a Distributed
System." Communications of the ACM, 21(7), 558-565, 1978.

2 The concept of causality in distributed systems was further developed by
Schwarz and Mattern in their work on vector clocks.
